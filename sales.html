Objection Handling

Questions CPAs
always ask.

Straight answers. No spin.

Trust / Credibility
I'm a CA Finalist with 4 years in a US-focused KPO firm serving US accounting clients directly. I've worked on tax advisory, bookkeeping, CFO-services accounts — not from theory but from doing the work. Your content will never read like it was written by a marketer who Googled "CPA tips." Ask me anything about depreciation schedules, 1031 exchanges, or quarterly estimates — I'll answer. That's the difference.
Skepticism
The reason LinkedIn doesn't work for most CPAs is posting without strategy — generic tips, no ICP targeting, no profile optimisation, no consistency. It's not the platform that failed. It's the approach. What I do is fundamentally different: every post has a purpose, every comment has a target, and every month has a measurable output. The system is what they were missing.
Stall
Completely fair. A couple of things worth considering before that conversation: spots in your niche are limited — I don't take competing clients, so the window matters. And the cost of staying invisible isn't zero — every month without LinkedIn is a month your competitors build authority you'd have to catch up to. What specific concern can I help you think through right now?
DIY objection
Absolutely — and many CPAs intend to. The question is: how's that been going? The challenge isn't knowledge, it's capacity. You have clients, deadlines, and a practice to run. LinkedIn rewards consistency above everything else — and consistency is the first thing that slips when you're busy. That's exactly the gap I fill. Your time is worth more doing billable work than writing LinkedIn posts.
Quality concern
Nothing goes live without your approval. Every single post comes to you first. If the tone is off, the angle isn't right, or you just don't like it — I revise until it's right. The first 2 weeks are a calibration period where I dial in your exact voice. After that, most clients rarely change anything. The goal is for your audience to never know someone else wrote it.
Price / ROI
Fair question. Let's do the math: one new mid-market client from LinkedIn could be worth $10,000–$50,000+ annually depending on your services. If this system produces one client every 3–6 months — which is a conservative estimate — the ROI is multiples. You're not spending on ads with uncertain reach. You're building permanent authority that compounds. Every post you publish today is an asset that works for years.
